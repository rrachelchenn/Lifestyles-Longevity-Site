About This Project

Future development, accessibility decisions, sustainability planning, and the technical foundations that make community-controlled health archives possible

From Proof of Concept to Community Resource

This archive began as a digital humanities final project critiquing Blue Zones mythology, but it's designed to become something larger: a sustainable, community-controlled platform for documenting diverse health experiences in the 21st century.

Current Status: Proof of concept with 10 archive items, demonstrating methodology and community engagement principles.

Vision: A longitudinal archive spanning decades, preserving authentic health narratives for future researchers while serving current community needs.

Omeka S Integration & Digital Infrastructure

This archive demonstrates the power of Omeka S as a platform for community-controlled digital collections. The project connects to a live Omeka.net archive: "How We Live Now: Modern Lifestyles in Archive"

While built as a custom website for accessibility, it showcases Omeka S principles and connects to the broader digital humanities infrastructure.

Accessibility Decisions & Implementation

Accessibility isn't an afterthoughtâ€”it's central to our mission of inclusive health documentation. Every design decision prioritizes multiple ways of accessing and contributing to the archive.

Digital Accessibility

Screen Reader Optimization

  • Semantic HTML structure with proper heading hierarchy
  • Alt-text for all images and data visualizations
  • ARIA labels for interactive elements
  • Skip navigation links for efficient browsing

Visual Accessibility

  • WCAG 2.1 AA color contrast compliance
  • Scalable text up to 200% without content loss
  • Multiple visual formats for same information
  • Clear visual hierarchy and consistent design patterns

Motor and Cognitive Accessibility

  • Keyboard-only navigation support
  • Sufficient click target sizes (44px minimum)
  • Clear language without unnecessary jargon
  • Multiple entry points for same content

Economic Accessibility

Free Access Commitment: No subscription fees, paywalls, or premium content tiers.

  • All archive content freely accessible without registration
  • Low-bandwidth design for limited internet access
  • Mobile-optimized for users who primarily access internet via phones
  • Downloadable content for offline access
  • Gift cards/appreciation payments for contributors, not fees

Cultural & Linguistic Accessibility

Community Language Priority: Respecting how people actually describe their health experiences.

  • Participants can contribute in their preferred language
  • Community-generated categories vs. imposed academic language
  • Browser translation guidance for non-English content
  • Cultural context preserved in metadata
  • Multiple cultural frameworks for understanding health

Privacy & Safety Accessibility

Participant Control: Multiple levels of sharing to accommodate different comfort levels.

  • Tiered consent: public, researchers only, community only
  • Pseudonym options for all contributors
  • Right to update or remove content at any time
  • No medical information required
  • Secure data storage with regular security audits

Sustainability & Sunset Planning

Ethical digital humanities projects must plan for their own end. This archive is designed to serve communities now while ensuring the data and relationships built here continue to benefit communities in the future.

Technical Sustainability

Data Preservation Strategy

  • Multiple Backup Locations: Cloud storage across different geographic regions
  • Format Migration Planning: Regular conversion to current standards
  • Metadata Standards: Dublin Core compliance for long-term discoverability
  • Partnership with University Libraries: Institutional repository integration

Platform Maintenance

  • Static site generation for reduced server dependencies
  • Open source codebase for community maintenance
  • Documentation for future technical stewards
  • Gradual transition to community-managed hosting

Community Ownership Transition

Governance Evolution

Years 1-3: Academic-led with community advisory board
Years 4-6: Shared governance with community co-leadership
Years 7+: Community-led with academic partnership

Knowledge Transfer

  • Archive management training for community members
  • Technical documentation in accessible language
  • Mentorship programs for next-generation stewards
  • Sustainable funding secured before academic transition

Educational Legacy

Replication Resources

  • Methodology Toolkit: Step-by-step guide for other communities
  • Technical Templates: Open source code for similar projects
  • Community Organizing Guide: Lessons learned about engagement
  • Scholarly Publications: Academic articles about approach and impact

Curriculum Integration

Ethical Sunset Protocols

Data Deletion Timeline

Participant Choice: All contributors can request data removal at any time

Scheduled Review: Every 5 years, active consent confirmation

Project End Notification: 2-year advance notice before any shutdown

Final Data Options: Transfer to community organization, institutional repository, or secure deletion

Community Transition Planning

  • Alternative platforms identified and tested
  • Community relationships maintained beyond digital archive
  • Participant network empowered for independent organizing
  • Lessons documented for future community archive projects

Positionality & Bias Statement

Following the example of community-controlled archives and transparency in research, this project acknowledges the perspectives and limitations that shape its approach.

Creator Background & Perspective

This archive was created by a graduate student in digital humanities with access to university resources, technical training, and academic networks. This position provides advantages (technical skills, institutional support) and limitations (potential academic bias, distance from some community experiences).

Health Experience: Personal motivation includes family experience with chronic conditions and frustration with wellness culture that ignores structural barriers to health.

Geographic Perspective: Bay Area focus reflects creator's location and networks, potentially missing health experiences from other regions, especially rural and non-coastal areas.

Institutional Context

Created within a university digital humanities program, this project benefits from academic freedom and resources while navigating institutional expectations about scholarship and community engagement.

Academic Constraints: Timeline driven by semester schedule rather than community needs; evaluation based on scholarly criteria not just community benefit.

Resource Access: Technical infrastructure and design support available through university connections not accessible to all community projects.

Community Relationship Commitments

This project commits to ongoing accountability to archive participants and transparent communication about how their contributions are used for both community benefit and academic advancement.

Ongoing Engagement: Archive participants have access to creator for questions, updates, and feedback on project direction.

Benefit Sharing: Any academic or professional opportunities resulting from this project include acknowledgment of community contributions and consideration of community benefit.

Limitations & Learning

This proof of concept has significant limitations that future iterations must address through deeper community partnership and expanded representation.

Current Limitations: Small sample size, English-language focus, Bay Area geographic restriction, limited technical accessibility testing with disabled users.

Learning Commitments: Ongoing education about community organizing, disability justice, multilingual digital design, and sustainable funding for community-controlled projects.

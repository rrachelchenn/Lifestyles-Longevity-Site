Why This Reading List Matters

This reading list goes beyond traditional academic citations to become a community resource. Each entry includes not just scholarly details, but explanations of why the work matters for understanding health, memory, and digital archives—written for both academic researchers and curious community members.

Following the example of community-controlled archives, this bibliography demonstrates how scholarship serves community needs rather than just academic requirements.

Introduction to Metadata

Murtha Baca 2016 Technical Guide
Baca, Murtha. "Introduction to Metadata." Getty Research Institute, Los Angeles, July 20, 2016. http://www.getty.edu/publications/intrometadata.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Essential for understanding how metadata choices shape user discovery and interpretation of archived materials.

For Community Members: Explains why the tags and categories we use to organize health stories actually affect which voices get heard and which get silenced.

For This Archive: Directly informs our decision to use community-generated tags rather than medical or library categories when organizing health experiences.

Key Concepts

Metadata as Argument Discoverability Politics Community-Controlled Vocabularies

Connects To

See how we apply these principles in our metadata explanation and interactive categorization demo.

The Making of Records

Michelle Caswell 2014 Scholarly Monograph
Caswell, Michelle. "The Making of Records." Archiving the Unspeakable: Silence, Memory, and the Photographic Record in Cambodia. Critical Human Rights. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press, 2014.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Foundational text on how archives are sites of power where some voices are preserved and others erased.

For Community Members: Helps understand why many health experiences—like night shift work, chronic conditions, or urban living—rarely show up in official health archives.

For This Archive: Motivates our emphasis on contemporary documentation and community involvement in record creation.

Key Concepts

Archives as Sites of Power Community Agency in Record Creation Documentation vs. Exploitation

Applied in This Archive

Our participant-controlled contribution process ensures that people maintain agency over their own health narratives rather than having their experiences interpreted by researchers.

Tell Me How It Ends: An Essay in Forty Questions

Valeria Luiselli 2017 Creative Nonfiction
Luiselli, Valeria. Tell Me How It Ends: An Essay in Forty Questions. Minneapolis: Coffee House Press, 2017.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Models ethical approaches to collecting and sharing personal stories, especially from vulnerable communities.

For Community Members: Shows how structured questioning can honor rather than extract from people's experiences.

For This Archive: Influences our interview methodology and commitment to participant agency in storytelling.

Key Concepts

Ethical Storytelling Questions as Framework Vulnerable Narrator Protection

Methodological Impact

Luiselli's approach to respectful questioning shapes our contribution guidelines, ensuring participants feel supported rather than interrogated when sharing health experiences.

The Power in the Story

Michel-Rolph Trouillot 2015 Historical Theory
Trouillot, Michel-Rolph, and Hazel V. Carby. "The Power in the Story." Silencing the Past: Power and the Production of History. Boston: Beacon Press, 2015.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Critical framework for understanding how dominant narratives shape what gets remembered and forgotten.

For Community Members: Explains why Blue Zones stories dominate health conversations while urban, working-class, and chronically ill experiences are marginalized.

For This Archive: Theoretical foundation for challenging Blue Zones mythology and centering diverse health narratives.

Key Concepts

Silences in Archives Narrative Power Counter-History

How We Apply This

Every archive decision—from whose stories we include to how we categorize experiences—either reinforces or challenges existing power structures in health research.

Queering the Map

Lucas LaRochelle Ongoing Digital Project
LaRochelle, Lucas. Queering the Map. https://www.queeringthemap.com/. Accessed 2025.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Demonstrates participatory mapping and community-controlled digital storytelling at scale.

For Community Members: Shows how personal experiences can create collective knowledge that challenges official narratives about place and identity.

For This Archive: Model for how communities can control their own representation in digital spaces.

Key Concepts

Participatory Mapping Community-Generated Content Anonymous Storytelling

Design Inspiration

Influences our approach to participatory metadata and community-driven organization of health experiences.

District Six Huis Kombuis: Food & Memory Cookbook

Tina Smith, ed. Shaun Viljoen 2016 Community Documentation
Smith, Tina. District Six Huis Kombuis: Food & Memory Cookbook. Edited by Shaun Viljoen. Cape Town: Quivertree Publications, 2016.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Demonstrates how food practices carry cultural memory and community knowledge through displacement and change.

For Community Members: Shows how recipes and food stories preserve identity and connection across generations.

For This Archive: Informs our documentation of cultural food adaptations and their health significance.

Key Concepts

Food as Cultural Memory Community Preservation Adaptation and Resilience

Connects to Our Archive

See our documentation of traditional recipe adaptations and cultural food knowledge.

Tenement Museum: Your Story, Our Story

Tenement Museum Ongoing Public History Project
Tenement Museum. "Your Story, Our Story" and Lesson Plans. https://www.tenement.org/lesson_plans/. Accessed 2025.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Model for connecting historical experiences to contemporary community concerns through public engagement.

For Community Members: Demonstrates how personal and family stories contribute to broader historical understanding.

For This Archive: Inspiration for making health archives accessible and relevant to diverse public audiences.

Key Concepts

Public History Immigrant Narratives Educational Access

Where We Differ

While we admire the Tenement Museum's public engagement, we prioritize free access and community control over our archive content, addressing the economic barriers that limit who can engage with cultural institutions.

The Elusive Archive in Material Culture Studies

Martin Brückner & Sandy Isenstadt 2021 Scholarly Essay
Brückner, Martin, and Sandy Isenstadt. "The Elusive Archive in Material Culture Studies." Elusive Archives: Material Culture Studies in Formation. Newark: University of Delaware Press, 2021.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Theoretical framework for understanding how everyday objects carry archival significance and cultural memory.

For Community Members: Validates the archival importance of daily health objects—from fitness trackers to home remedies to community garden tools.

For This Archive: Supports our inclusion of photos, material culture documentation, and everyday health artifacts.

Key Concepts

Material Archives Everyday Object Significance Cultural Memory in Things

SAA Dictionary of Archives Terminology

Society of American Archivists Ongoing Professional Standards
Society of American Archivists. Definitions of "Archives," "Record," "Document," "Papers," "Value," and "Content," "Context," and "Structure." SAA Dictionary. https://dictionary.archivists.org/.

Why This Matters

For Researchers: Professional standards that define what counts as archival material and how value is determined.

For Community Members: Understanding these definitions helps recognize why personal health stories have archival significance.

For This Archive: Foundational concepts that we both follow and expand to include community-defined value systems.
